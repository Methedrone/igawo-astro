# Common Mistakes in German Tax Returns – What to Avoid

Filing a German tax return isn't rocket science, but it's full of traps. One wrong click, one unread note, and instead of a refund you get a rejection. Or worse – a demand for back taxes.

After years of working with hundreds of returns, I've seen the same mistakes again and again. In this article, I describe the most common ones and explain how to avoid them.

## Mistake 1: Missing Lohnabrechnungen

Lohnabrechnung is your sacred right. It's the document showing how much you earned, how much tax your employer paid, and what social contributions were deducted. Without it, the tax office has no proof of your income.

**How to avoid:**

- Always demand Lohnabrechnung from your employer
- Keep all documents for at least 5 years
- If you don't have them, we can recover them from your employer

**What happens without Lohnabrechnungen:**

The tax office may reject your return or issue an estimated assessment, which is usually unfavorable.

## Mistake 2: Not Reporting Home-Country Income

Many foreigners think: "Since I worked in Germany, why do the Germans need to know about my home-country earnings?" Wrong. The German tax office must know all your income in a given tax year.

Germany and most countries have double-taxation treaties. This doesn't mean you don't report home-country income. It means you won't pay tax twice. But both countries need the full picture.

**How to avoid:**

- Always report home-country income in your German return (Anlage AUS)
- Keep your home-country tax documents and earnings certificates
- If you don't have home-country documents, we can obtain them

**Consequences of hiding income:**

Tax fraud in Germany is a crime. It can result in fines and, in extreme cases, even prison. Better to report everything honestly.

## Mistake 3: Wrong Bank Account Number

Your refund goes to the account you provide. If you enter a wrong IBAN, the money may end up with someone else. Recovering it takes months.

**How to avoid:**

- Always double-check your IBAN
- Make sure the account is active
- If you don't have a German account, provide your home-country IBAN – refunds to EU accounts are possible

## Mistake 4: Missing Travel Deductions

This is one of the costliest mistakes. Many workers don't know they can deduct commuting costs. With a 20 km commute, that's about EUR 1,300 per year.

**How to avoid:**

- Record your mileage – how many kilometers daily, which route
- Keep public transport tickets
- If you drove, record fuel costs

**Note**: the tax office requires the cheapest available transport method. If you drove but could have taken the train for less, the deduction may be limited.

## Mistake 5: Wrong Marital Filing Status

In Germany, married couples can file jointly (Zusammenveranlagung) or separately (Getrenntveranlagung). Joint filing is usually better, but not always.

**Example**: wife doesn't work, husband earns EUR 40,000. Joint filing lowers tax through income splitting. But if both earn similar amounts, separate filing may be better.

**How to avoid:**

- Calculate both options
- Consult an accountant
- Don't assume joint is always better

## Mistake 6: Missing Steuer-ID

Steuer-ID is an 11-digit number you receive upon first contact with the German tax office. Without it, filing is significantly harder.

**How to avoid:**

- Save your Steuer-ID immediately upon receiving it
- You'll find it on tax notices, employer documents, or tax office correspondence
- If you don't have one, we can obtain it on your behalf

## Mistake 7: Missing Accommodation Deductions

If you rented in Germany while your permanent home is abroad, you're entitled to accommodation deductions. Many workers don't know this.

**What you can deduct:**

- Rent
- Electricity, water, gas bills
- Home insurance
- Internet fees (if needed for work)

**How to avoid:**

- Keep all invoices and rental contracts
- Record the periods you rented accommodation
- Provide your full home-country address as permanent residence

## Mistake 8: Wrong Dates in the Return

The German tax year is the calendar year: 1 January to 31 December. Many foreigners enter dates from contracts that don't align with the calendar year.

**Example**: you worked from 15 March to 30 November. In the return, you must enter exact dates. The tax office uses them to proportionally calculate allowances and refunds.

**How to avoid:**

- Check dates on contracts and Lohnabrechnungen
- Make sure all months are covered
- If you worked for multiple employers, create a table with dates

## Mistake 9: Missing the Filing Deadline

The deadline is 31 July of the following year (or end of February if using an accountant). After the deadline, penalties apply.

But if you're owed a refund, you can often file after the deadline. In that case, no penalties apply. However, the tax office may refuse the return if more than 4 years have passed.

**How to avoid:**

- File as soon as possible
- Don't wait until the last minute
- If you're running out of time, contact us – we can speed things up

## Mistake 10: Filling Out Forms Without Knowing the Language

German tax forms are in German. Even if you use Polish or English software, translations are often imprecise. One misunderstood word can change the meaning of an entire section.

**How to avoid:**

- If you don't know German, get help
- Don't rely solely on Google Translate
- Make sure you understand every field you fill out

## Summary – Checklist

Before submitting your return, check:

- [ ] I have all Lohnabrechnungen
- [ ] I reported home-country income
- [ ] My bank account number is correct
- [ ] I entered travel costs
- [ ] I chose the right marital filing status
- [ ] I have my Steuer-ID
- [ ] I entered accommodation costs (if applicable)
- [ ] Employment dates are correct
- [ ] The return is filed on time
- [ ] I understand all filled fields
