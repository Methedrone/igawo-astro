# German and Dutch Tax Forms – How to Fill Them Out Without Stress

German and Dutch tax forms terrify many foreign workers. Hundreds of fields, incomprehensible abbreviations, stern warnings about penalties for mistakes. But in practice, most people only need to fill out a few sections. The rest are attachments for specific situations.

In this article, I explain the most important forms step by step. No legal jargon, just plain language.

## German Tax Forms

### Mantelbogen (EB1) – Cover Page

This is the most important form. Without a correctly filled Mantelbogen, your return is invalid.

**Fields you must fill:**

- **Steuer-ID** – 11-digit tax identification number
- **Registered address** – if you don't have a permanent German address, enter your last known one
- **Marital status** – choose the right option (ledig = single, verheiratet = married)
- **Religion** – enter "oa" (ohne Angabe = none) or select the correct one. This matters because it affects church tax
- **Bank details** – IBAN for the refund
- **Date of birth** – in DD.MM.YYYY format

**Most common mistake**: entering the wrong Steuer-ID. Triple-check it.

### Anlage N – Income from Employment

Here you copy data from your Lohnabrechnungen:

- **Annual gross** – total of all earnings
- **Lohnsteuer** – tax paid by employer
- **Soli** – solidarity surcharge
- **Kirchensteuer** – church tax (if applicable)
- **Social security contributions** – Rentenversicherung, Krankenversicherung, Pflegeversicherung, Arbeitslosenversicherung

**Tip**: all this data is on your last Lohnabrechnung of the year or your annual certificate (Lohnsteuerbescheinigung).

### Anlage AUS – Foreign Income

If you also worked in your home country in the same tax year, you must fill this out. Enter:

- Home-country employer name
- Earnings amount in local currency and converted to euros
- Tax paid in your home country

**Note**: if you don't report home-country income, you're committing tax fraud. The German tax office exchanges information with many countries.

### Anlage EKS – Travel and Accommodation Costs

This is crucial for posted workers:

- **Travel costs** – number of kilometers × 0.30 euro (from 2024)
- **Accommodation costs** – monthly rent × number of months
- **Meal costs** – if applicable
- **Other work expenses** – clothing, tools, phone

### Anlage V – Joint Filing for Spouses

If you're married and want to file jointly:

- Spouse details
- Spouse income
- Joint expenses

**Tip**: joint filing is usually more beneficial, but it's worth checking.

### Anlage KAP – Capital Income

If you have interest from savings, dividends, or other capital gains, report them here.

## Dutch Tax Forms

### Aangifte inkomstenbelasting – Income Tax Return

The Dutch tax return is simpler than the German one. It consists of several numbered sections.

#### Section 1 – Personal Details

- **BSN** – 9-digit tax identification number
- **Address** – current residence
- **Bank details** – IBAN

#### Section 3 – Employment Income

Copy data from your jaaropgave:

- **Loon loonheffing** – gross salary
- **Ingehouden loonbelasting** – tax paid
- **Ingehouden premie volksverzekeringen** – social security premiums
- **Werkgeversheffing** – employer contributions

#### Section 4 – Tax Deductions

- **Heffingskorting** – basic tax credit
- **Arbeidskorting** – employed person's tax credit
- **Algemene heffingskorting** – general tax credit

**Tip**: most credits are applied automatically by your employer, but when filing your annual return you can claim corrections.

#### Section 9 – Foreign Income

If you also worked outside the Netherlands, report it here.

## How to Fill Out Forms So They Don't Get Rejected

### Rule 1: Legibility

If filing on paper, use block capitals. Don't lean over the form – officials must read every digit.

### Rule 2: Accuracy

Every amount must be exact. Don't round, don't estimate. Use precise figures from your documents.

### Rule 3: Consistency

Data in the return must match your documents. If Lohnabrechnung shows gross of EUR 28,450.67, enter exactly that. Even a small difference triggers questions.

### Rule 4: Completeness

Don't skip attachments. If deducting travel costs, attach a ticket copy or mileage calculation. If deducting insurance, attach the policy.

### Rule 5: Timeliness

German deadline: 31 July (or end of February with an accountant). Dutch deadline: 1 May (or 1 September with an accountant).

## Frequently Asked Questions About Forms

### Do I have to fill out all attachments?

No. Only fill out those that apply to your situation. If you have no capital income, don't fill out Anlage KAP.

### Can I fill out forms online?

Germany: yes, through ELSTER, but it requires a German identifier or certificate. For foreigners, paper filing or using an accountant is easiest.

Netherlands: yes, through Mijn Belastingdienst, but it requires DigiD – a Dutch digital identifier.

### What if I don't understand a question?

Don't guess. Every mistake can cost you your refund or trigger a penalty. Consult an accountant or ask for translation.

### Can I correct a filed return?

Yes. You can file a correction (Berichtigung). But the process is long and can delay your refund by months.

## Summary

Filling out German and Dutch tax forms requires precision, but it's not impossible. Main rules:

- Start with Mantelbogen / Section 1 – personal details must be perfect
- Copy exact amounts from source documents
- Don't guess – if you don't understand, ask
- Attach all supporting documents
- Keep a copy of the return
- Don't miss the deadline
