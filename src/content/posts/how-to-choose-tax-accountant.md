# How to Choose a Tax Accountant for Foreign Tax Returns

Looking for an accountant to help you file a tax return from Germany or the Netherlands? Be careful. The tax services market is full of agencies that promise mountains of gold, then disappear with your documents or send a bill much higher than agreed.

After years in the industry, I know what clients should look for. In this article, I share practical tips on how to choose an accountant who actually helps instead of creating problems.

## Do You Even Need an Accountant?

Let's start with the basics. If you have a simple case – one employer, one country, no children, standard expenses – you might try filing yourself. Software like ELSTER or WISO Steuer lets you file online.

But if:

- You worked for multiple employers
- You have children and want to claim family allowances
- You want to deduct travel, accommodation, or other expenses
- You worked in multiple countries in the same year
- You received a rejection and want to appeal

…it's worth getting help. The difference in your refund often exceeds the service cost many times over.

## What Should a Foreign Tax Accountant Know?

Not every accountant knows foreign tax rules. Not every German Steuerberater understands the specifics of foreign workers. Look for someone who:

### 1. Knows the relevant country's tax law

If you're filing for Germany, your accountant must know Einkommensteuergesetz, BUrlG, and BFH case law. If the Netherlands, they must understand Wet IB, loonheffing, and Belastingdienst rules.

**Test question**: "Which German tax attachments do we fill out for accommodation costs during a posting?" If the accountant doesn't know what you're talking about – keep looking.

### 2. Has experience with foreign workers

Filing for a foreign worker is different from filing for a local. Different allowances, different problems, different correspondence with the tax office. Your accountant must understand posting work, double residence, and double-taxation treaties.

**Test question**: "How do we handle income from two countries in the same tax year?" A good accountant answers immediately, without hesitation.

### 3. Speaks your language and the local language

This seems obvious, but many firms employ people who only speak your language. Then correspondence with the German tax office goes through a translator or doesn't happen at all. And correspondence is crucial – the tax office often asks for clarification, and failing to respond means rejection.

**Check**: does the firm independently handle correspondence with Finanzamt in German?

### 4. Is available after the service ends

Many firms prepare the return, take payment, and vanish. Then a letter arrives from the tax office with questions, an appeal, or a correction – and there's no one to call.

**Question**: "Will you be available if the tax office sends additional questions?" If the answer is "that's outside the service scope" – run.

## Red Flags to Watch For

### No fixed office address

If the accountant only accepts documents by courier, operates from a "virtual office," or has no fixed address – that's a risk. If problems arise, there's nowhere to turn.

### Promises without basis

"We guarantee a EUR 5,000 refund!" – impossible. No one can guarantee a specific amount without seeing your documents. A good accountant says: "I need to see your documents to estimate the refund."

### No contract

If the accountant doesn't sign a contract, doesn't issue an invoice, or works "off the books" – that's a warning sign. A professional always documents the engagement.

### 100% upfront payment

The standard is a deposit or payment after service completion. If someone demands the full amount before doing anything – be cautious.

### No references

A new firm with no track record is a risk. Look for online reviews, ask friends. Even one positive reference from someone you know is worth more than five anonymous reviews.

## How Much Should a Tax Accountant Cost?

Costs vary by city and case complexity:

- Simple German return: EUR 80–150
- Return with children and expenses: EUR 150–250
- Complex case (multiple employers, appeals): EUR 250–400
- Dutch return: EUR 80–150

In Germany, accountant fees are fully tax-deductible. This means in practice you only pay about 60–70% of the net amount.

## What Does Working with a Good Accountant Look Like?

1. **Initial consultation** – you discuss your situation, the accountant estimates refund and cost
2. **Signing a contract** – clear terms, price, scope of services
3. **Submitting documents** – in person, by courier, or online
4. **Preparing the return** – the accountant fills out the forms
5. **Review** – they show you the return before submission
6. **Filing** – they submit to the tax office
7. **Office correspondence** – they answer questions, handle communication
8. **Refund** – money arrives in your account

The whole process usually takes 2 to 8 weeks, depending on the office and case complexity.

## Questions to Ask Before Signing

1. How many German/Dutch returns did you do last year?
2. Do you handle correspondence with the tax office in German?
3. What's the estimated turnaround time?
4. What happens if the office rejects the return or sends questions?
5. Is an appeal included in the price if needed?
6. What documents are needed, and what if something is missing?
7. Will I get a copy of the filed return?
8. What are the payment terms?

## Summary

Choosing a tax accountant is an important decision. A good accountant doesn't just prepare the return – they also:

- Help you get the maximum refund
- Answer tax office questions
- Advise on future filings
- Protect you from mistakes and penalties

A bad accountant creates problems, disappears with your money, or leaves you alone with a rejection letter.
