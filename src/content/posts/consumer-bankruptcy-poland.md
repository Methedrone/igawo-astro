# Consumer Bankruptcy in Poland – Step-by-Step Guide

Do you have debts you can't repay? Has a bailiff seized part of your wages, and debt collectors call daily? You're not alone. In Poland, hundreds of thousands of people are in a similar situation. But there is a solution – consumer bankruptcy.

In this article, I explain step by step what consumer bankruptcy is, who can declare it, how the procedure works, and what happens after it ends.

## What Is Consumer Bankruptcy?

Consumer bankruptcy is a legal procedure that allows individuals (who don't run a business) to free themselves from debt. Introduced in 2015, it replaced earlier rules that were much less favorable to debtors.

**Basic principle**: if you can't meet your obligations and your financial situation doesn't allow you to satisfy creditors, you can declare bankruptcy. After the procedure ends, most debts are canceled.

## Who Can Declare Consumer Bankruptcy?

Not everyone qualifies. You must meet several conditions:

### 1. Be an individual not running a business

Consumer bankruptcy applies to people who don't run a company. If you have a business, different rules apply – business bankruptcy.

### 2. Be insolvent

Insolvency means you can't meet your current obligations. It's not about having difficulties – it's about actually being unable to pay.

### 3. Have assets exceeding procedure costs

Your assets must be worth more than the cost of the procedure itself. If you have no assets at all, the court may refuse bankruptcy.

### 4. Not have declared bankruptcy in the last 10 years

If you've already gone through consumer bankruptcy, you must wait 10 years before filing again.

### 5. Not have caused insolvency intentionally or through gross negligence

If you deliberately ran up debts knowing you couldn't repay them, the court may refuse. This applies, for example, to taking out loans right before filing for bankruptcy.

## Which Debts Are Covered?

Most debts are canceled after consumer bankruptcy. These include:

- Bank loans
- Non-bank loans
- Credit card debt
- Unpaid bills (electricity, gas, phone)
- Child support arrears
- Fines
- Tax and ZUS arrears

**Debts that are NOT canceled:**

- Current child support (you must keep paying)
- Compensation for crimes (e.g., drunk driving accident)
- Some court-ordered penalties

## How Does the Procedure Work?

### Step 1: Application to court

You submit your application to the district court for your place of residence. The application must include:

- Description of your financial situation
- List of creditors with debt amounts
- List of assets (property, car, savings)
- Income information
- Explanation of how the debt arose
- Proposed repayment plan (if you can partially repay)

**Note**: you can file yourself, but given the complexity of the rules, it's worth getting legal help.

### Step 2: Bankruptcy proceedings

The court appoints a trustee – a person who manages your assets. The trustee:

- Reviews documentation
- Sells assets that can be liquidated
- Distributes funds among creditors
- Supervises the repayment plan (if one is established)

During this time, bailiff enforcement and debt collection are suspended.

### Step 3: Repayment plan or liquidation

The court may order one of three solutions:

#### a) Repayment plan

You agree with creditors on a partial repayment plan. If creditors agree, you pay the agreed amount for a set period (usually 3–5 years), and the rest of the debt is canceled.

#### b) Asset liquidation

If repayment isn't possible, the trustee sells your assets (except basic necessities) and distributes the money among creditors.

#### c) No assets to repay

If you have no assets and can't repay even partially, the court may end the proceedings without a repayment plan.

### Step 4: End of proceedings

After completing the repayment plan or asset liquidation, the court issues a decision ending the bankruptcy. From that point, remaining debts are canceled.

## What Happens to Your Assets?

You don't lose everything. The court leaves you with:

- Your home (if it's your only one and of modest value)
- Basic household equipment
- Clothing and personal items
- Means of transport (within reason)
- Savings up to a certain limit

But you will lose:

- Investment properties
- Expensive cars (above a certain value)
- Savings above the limit
- Valuables

## How Long Does Consumer Bankruptcy Take?

It depends on the situation:

- **With repayment plan**: 3–5 years
- **With asset liquidation**: 6–12 months
- **Without assets**: 3–6 months

After the proceedings, a "stain" remains in credit databases for several more years. This makes getting new loans harder, but it doesn't make credit impossible.

## Is It Worth Declaring Bankruptcy?

It's a difficult decision. On one hand, bankruptcy gives you a "second chance," frees you from debt, and stops the downward spiral. On the other hand, it involves losing some assets and future difficulties in obtaining credit.

**Bankruptcy is worth considering when:**

- Your debts are growing despite repayments
- A bailiff seizes most of your wages
- You see no realistic chance of repayment in the coming years
- Debt-related stress affects your health and family

**Not worth it when:**

- You have temporary problems but prospects for improvement
- The debt is small and you can repay it within a year or two
- Your debts are mainly child support (these remain anyway)

## How Much Does Consumer Bankruptcy Cost?

Costs vary:

- **Court fee**: PLN 30 to 1,000 (depending on asset value)
- **Trustee fee**: several hundred to several thousand PLN
- **Legal help**: PLN 1,000 to 5,000

People with low incomes can apply for exemption from court costs or court-appointed legal aid.

## Summary

Consumer bankruptcy is a tool that lets you start over. Key points:

- Anyone who is insolvent and doesn't run a business can declare it
- Most debts are canceled after the procedure ends
- The procedure takes from a few months to a few years
- You don't lose everything – you keep basic assets
- Costs vary, but fee exemptions are available

If you're considering bankruptcy, don't decide hastily. Consult a lawyer who will analyze your situation.
