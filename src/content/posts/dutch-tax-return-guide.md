# Dutch Tax Return Guide – Belastingdienst for Foreign Workers

The Netherlands is one of the most popular destinations for foreign workers. Jobs in Amsterdam, Rotterdam, and The Hague attract hundreds of thousands of people every year. But did you know that, just like in Germany, you probably overpaid tax and can get it back?

In this article, I explain how the Dutch tax system works, what loonheffing is, what deductions you're entitled to, and how to avoid the most common mistakes when filing a Dutch tax return.

## How Does Tax Work in the Netherlands?

The Dutch tax system differs from many others. The key difference is that your employer doesn't pay you a net salary. Instead, they withhold income tax – **loonheffing** – as an advance payment on your annual tax.

In practice, it works like this:

- Your employer calculates your gross salary
- Deducts social security premiums
- Deducts tax advance (loonheffing)
- Pays the remainder to your account

The problem is that loonheffing is calculated upfront – assuming you'll work the entire year at that rate. If you worked shorter, earned less, had a break, or returned home, the advance was too high.

## Who Can File a Tax Return in the Netherlands?

You're eligible if you:

- Worked legally in the Netherlands under an employment contract
- Have a Dutch tax identification number (BSN)
- Received a year-end statement (jaaropgave)
- Haven't passed the filing deadline

**Deadlines matter**: you have 5 years to file for any given year. This means in 2025, you can still file for 2020–2024.

### Does agency work count?

Yes. If you worked through a staffing agency (uitzendbureau), you can still file. In this case, your annual statement comes from the agency, not the company where you physically worked.

## Documents Needed for a Dutch Tax Return

Without the right documents, you can't start. Here's the list:

### Essential documents:

1. **Jaaropgave** – annual earnings statement from your employer
2. **BSN (Burgerservicenummer)** – Dutch tax identification number
3. **Copies of employment contracts** – especially if you changed employers
4. **Bank details** – IBAN for the refund

### Additional documents that increase your refund:

- Travel expense receipts (kilometervergoeding)
- Accommodation costs
- Vocational training invoices
- Childcare costs
- Health insurance in your home country (if you didn't use Dutch insurance)

## How to Fill Out a Dutch Tax Return

The Dutch tax return consists of several sections. The most important:

### Section 1 – personal details

- **BSN** – 9-digit tax identification number
- **Address** – current residence
- **Bank details** – IBAN

### Section 3 – employment income

Copy data from your jaaropgave:

- **Loon loonheffing** – gross salary
- **Ingehouden loonbelasting** – tax paid
- **Ingehouden premie volksverzekeringen** – social security premiums
- **Werkgeversheffing** – employer contributions

### Section 4 – tax deductions

- **Heffingskorting** – basic tax credit
- **Arbeidskorting** – employed person's tax credit
- **Algemene heffingskorting** – general tax credit

**Tip**: most credits are applied automatically by your employer, but when filing your annual return, you can claim corrections.

### Section 9 – foreign income

If you also worked outside the Netherlands in the same year, report it here.

## How Much Can You Get Back?

Typical Dutch refunds range from EUR 300 to 2,000 per year. It depends on:

- How many months you worked
- What your salary was
- Whether you claimed deductions
- Whether you also worked in another country

**Example**: if you worked 6 months at EUR 2,200 net per month, your refund could be around EUR 800–1,200. If you worked the full year but part-time, the refund could be even higher.

## Common Mistakes When Filing a Dutch Tax Return

After years of experience, I see the same mistakes:

### 1. Missing jaaropgave

Many workers don't know they need to ask their employer for an annual statement. Without it, you can't file.

### 2. Not reporting home-country income

Belastingdienst requires honesty. If you also worked in your home country, you must report it. The Netherlands and most countries have double-taxation treaties, so you won't pay twice, but the tax office needs to know all your income.

### 3. Wrong BSN number

A BSN is 9 digits. One wrong digit means your refund goes to someone else or gets blocked.

### 4. No Dutch bank account

Belastingdienst pays refunds to Dutch accounts. If you've closed yours, provide a foreign IBAN. This works but may take longer.

### 5. Wrong marital filing choice

If you're married, you can file individually or jointly. The wrong choice can cost you hundreds of euros.

## Dutch Tax Deadlines

The filing deadline is **1 May** of the following year. If you use a tax advisor, you have until **1 September**.

If you're owed a refund, you can usually file after the deadline without penalties. Belastingdienst doesn't charge late fees when they owe you money.

For underpayments, the situation is different – then penalties are high.

The Netherlands is an attractive place to work, but don't forget about your tax return. Key points:

- Most people who work in the Netherlands overpay tax
- Typical refunds range from EUR 300 to 2,000 per year
- You have 5 years to file
- Basic credits are heffingskorting and arbeidskorting
- Missing documents can be recovered from your employer
