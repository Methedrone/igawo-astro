# German Tax Deductions – What Foreign Workers Can Claim

When you work in Germany, you pay taxes. But did you know that the German system allows you to deduct many costs you might not even have noticed? Many foreigners lose hundreds, often thousands of euros per year simply because they don't know their rights.

In this article, I present a complete overview of deductions and allowances available to workers from abroad. No legal jargon – just clear, practical information.

## Why Deductions Matter

The German tax system is built so that tax is calculated on the income that actually stays in your pocket. This means all costs related to work, life, and family situation can lower your tax bill.

**Example**: you earn EUR 35,000 per year. Without deductions, you'd pay around EUR 5,000 in tax. But if you have commuting costs (EUR 2,000), accommodation costs (EUR 3,000), and insurance (EUR 1,500) – your taxable income drops to EUR 28,500, and tax falls to around EUR 3,500. The difference is EUR 1,500 in your pocket.

That's real money. These aren't tricks or loopholes – they're normal, legal deductions that millions of Germans use.

## Werbungskosten – Work-Related Expenses

This is the most important category. It covers everything you spend to earn your income.

### Travel to Work (Fahrtkosten)

You can deduct commuting costs. From 2024, the rate is 30 cents per kilometer one way. So if you commuted 15 km to work, that's EUR 9 per day. At 220 workdays per year, that's EUR 1,980 in deductions.

**What you can deduct:**

- Fuel costs (if driving)
- Public transport tickets
- Train tickets
- Partial car insurance costs

**Note**: you must indicate the cheapest available transport option. If you drove but could have taken the train for less, the deduction may be limited.

### Second Residence (Zweitwohnsitz)

If you rented in Germany while your permanent home is abroad, you can deduct accommodation costs:

- Rent
- Electricity and water bills
- Home insurance
- Internet fees

The maximum deduction is around EUR 1,000 per month, depending on your situation.

### Work Clothing and Tools (Arbeitskleidung, Werkzeug)

If you had to buy special clothing, safety boots, helmets, protective glasses, or tools – all of this is deductible. Condition: these must be items you don't use privately.

### Accountant Fees (Steuerberaterkosten)

The cost of preparing your tax return is fully deductible. This means in practice your accountant only costs about 60–70% of the invoice amount.

### Training and Courses (Fortbildungskosten)

If you attended vocational training, language courses (if necessary for work), or other qualification upgrades – costs are deductible.

### Bank Account Fees (Kontoführungsgebühren)

If the account is used to receive salary, the fees are deductible. Up to around EUR 50 per year.

## Sonderausgaben – Special Expenses

These are costs not directly related to work but affecting your life situation:

### Insurance

Germany allows deductions for many types of insurance:

- Health insurance (Krankenversicherung) – partially
- Pension insurance (Rentenversicherung) – partially
- Unemployment insurance (Arbeitslosenversicherung)
- Life insurance (Lebensversicherung) – under conditions
- Liability insurance (Haftpflichtversicherung)

### Donations (Spenden)

Donations to charities, churches, and political parties are deductible. Up to 20% of income.

### Care Costs (Pflegekosten)

If you care for a disabled person, related costs are deductible.

### Maintenance Payments (Unterhalt)

If you pay maintenance to a former spouse or partner, these amounts are deductible under conditions.

## Außergewöhnliche Belastungen – Extraordinary Burdens

These apply to people in difficult life situations. They cover:

- Medical treatment and surgery
- Purchase of glasses, hearing aids, prosthetics
- Nursing and care costs
- Rehabilitation

Condition: costs must be "extraordinary," meaning significantly higher than normal. The tax office considers your income and determines how much you can deduct.

## Family Allowances

Have children? Additional options open up:

### Kinderfreibetrag (Child Tax Allowance)

In 2024, this is EUR 6,264 for the first and second child, EUR 6,390 for the third, and EUR 7,260 for the fourth and each additional child. This amount is deducted from your taxable income.

**Important**: the tax office automatically checks whether Kinderfreibetrag or Kindergeld is better for you. Usually Kindergeld is more favorable, but for higher incomes Kinderfreibetrag may be better.

### Spouse Allowances (Ehegattensplitting)

If you're married and file jointly, the basic tax-free amount is EUR 22,848 (for both, in 2024). That's double the individual amount.

### Child School Commute Costs

You can deduct up to EUR 130 per month for your child's school commute if there's no school nearby.

### Childcare Costs (Kinderbetreuungskosten)

Daycare, kindergarten, babysitter, or camp costs are deductible up to EUR 6,000 per year per child.

## Basic Tax-Free Allowance

In 2024, the basic tax-free amount (Grundfreibetrag) is EUR 11,604. This means the first EUR 11,604 of your income is tax-free. If you earn less, you pay no income tax at all.

## Are Deductions Available to Foreigners?

Yes. As an EU citizen, you have the same rights to tax deductions as Germans. There are no citizenship-based restrictions.

The only limitation: some deductions require German registration. If you worked short-term and weren't registered, some deductions may be harder to claim. But commuting costs, insurance, and basic allowances apply regardless of registration.

## How to Claim Deductions on Your Return

All deductions are claimed in your tax return (Einkommensteuererklärung). Key attachments:

- **Anlage N** – employment income
- **Anlage EKS** – work-related expenses
- **Anlage Sonderausgaben** – special expenses
- **Anlage AUS** – foreign income
- **Anlage V** – joint filing for spouses

Not sure which attachments to fill out? That's normal. Most people need help with their first return.

## Summary

The German tax system offers many ways to save. Main deduction categories:

- **Work-related expenses**: travel, accommodation, work clothing, accountant
- **Special expenses**: insurance, donations, maintenance
- **Extraordinary burdens**: medical, rehabilitation, care
- **Family allowances**: child allowance, childcare, school commute
