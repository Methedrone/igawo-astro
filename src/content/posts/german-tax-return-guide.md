# How to File a German Tax Return – Step-by-Step Guide

Worked in Germany and wondering if you're owed a tax refund? You're not alone. Every year, hundreds of thousands of foreigners head to Germany for work, and most overpay income tax. This article walks you through how to file a German tax return step by step, how much you might get back, and what to watch out for.

## Is It Worth Filing a German Tax Return?

Short answer: yes, absolutely. The German tax system assumes your employer withholds income tax each month based on an annual rate. That rate assumes you'll work the entire year for the same employer at the same salary.

In reality, that rarely happens. Did you return to your home country before year-end? Change employers? Work only a few months? In every one of these cases, you likely overpaid tax.

**Refund amounts vary**, but typically range from a few hundred to several thousand euros. Many people receive refunds between EUR 800 and 2,500 per year. That's real money sitting in the German tax office waiting for your claim.

## Who Can File a Tax Return from Germany?

Not everyone is eligible, but most people who worked legally in Germany can file. Basic requirements:

- You worked under an employment contract (Arbeitsvertrag)
- You received German payroll documents (Lohnabrechnungen)
- You have a German tax identification number (Steuer-ID)
- You haven't passed the deadline (usually 4 years back)

**Important**: even if you worked through an agency or on a temporary contract, you can still file. As long as you were employed under German terms, you're covered.

## What Documents Do You Need?

Before you start filling out forms, make sure you have all required documents. Missing Lohnabrechnungen or a Steuer-ID can delay your refund by months.

### Essential documents:

1. **Lohnabrechnungen (payroll statements)** – you should receive these from your employer. If you don't have them, we can request them on your behalf.
2. **Steuerbescheid (tax assessment notice)** – if you've filed before, this shows what you paid and whether you received a refund.
3. **Steuer-ID (tax ID)** – an 11-digit number. You'll find it on your tax notice or employer's documents.
4. **Identity documents** – passport or national ID card.
5. **Bank details** – IBAN where the refund should be sent.

### Additional documents that increase your refund:

- Travel expenses to work (Fahrkosten)
- Accommodation costs in Germany (for posted workers)
- Invoices for work clothing or tools
- Health insurance costs in your home country (if not in the German system)
- Proof of contributions to savings accounts or life insurance

Unsure which expenses you can deduct? Don't worry – that's one of the most common questions. The next section covers deductions in detail.

## What Tax Deductions Are Available?

The German tax system offers many deductions that foreign workers often don't know about. Using them correctly can increase your refund by hundreds of euros.

### Werbungskosten (work-related expenses)

This is the most important deduction category. It covers everything you spend to earn your income:

- **Travel to work** – you can deduct 30 cents per kilometer (from 2024). If you commuted 20 km daily for 200 workdays, that's EUR 1,200 in deductions.
- **Accommodation costs** – if you rented in Germany while your permanent home is abroad, you can deduct up to EUR 1,000 per month.
- **Second household** – if your family lives in your home country and you work in Germany, this deduction applies.
- **Work clothing and tools** – special gear you don't use privately.
- **Accountant fees** – the cost of preparing your tax return is fully deductible.

### Sonderausgaben (special expenses)

These are costs not directly related to work but affecting your life situation:

- **Insurance** – health, pension, unemployment, and life insurance premiums.
- **Donations** – to charities, churches, political parties.
- **Bank account fees** – if the account is used to receive salary.

### Außergewöhnliche Belastungen (extraordinary burdens)

These apply to difficult life situations – illness, disability, care costs, medical equipment, glasses, prosthetics.

### Family allowances

If you have children, additional options open up:

- **Kinderfreibetrag** – child tax allowance (EUR 6,264 per child in 2024).
- **Spouse allowances** – when filing jointly, the tax-free amount doubles.
- **Childcare costs** – up to EUR 6,000 per year per child.

## How to Fill Out the German Tax Return

A German tax return consists of several sections. The most important are:

### Mantelbogen (EB1)

The cover page. You enter personal details, address, marital status, tax ID, and bank details. **Important**: a mistake here can get your entire return rejected.

### Anlage N (income from employment)

Enter data from your Lohnabrechnungen: gross salary, taxes paid by employer, social security contributions.

### Anlage AUS (foreign income)

If you also worked in your home country in the same year, you must report it here. The German tax office needs to know about all your income.

### Anlage V (joint filing for spouses)

If you're married and want to file jointly with your spouse, complete this section. For many couples, joint filing is more beneficial.

### Anlage EKS (travel and accommodation costs)

Enter all work-related travel and housing expenses. This is crucial for posted workers.

### Anlage KAP (capital income)

If you have interest from savings, dividends, or other capital gains, report them here.

DIY filing is technically free, but:

- Software costs around EUR 20–40
- A mistake can cost you your refund or even trigger penalties
- Correcting a return is possible but significantly delays your refund

## Common Mistakes When Filing a German Tax Return

After years of experience with hundreds of returns, here are the mistakes I see most often:

### 1. Missing Lohnabrechnungen

Many workers don't keep their payroll statements. Without them, the tax office has no proof of your earnings. **Tip**: always keep all documents. If you don't have them, we can recover them from your employer.

### 2. Not reporting home-country income

If you worked in your home country in the same year, you must report it. Hiding this income is tax fraud. The German tax office can exchange information with tax authorities in other countries.

### 3. Wrong bank account number

The refund goes to the account you provide. If you enter a wrong IBAN, the money may go to someone else. Always double-check your account details.

### 4. Missing travel deductions

Many workers don't know they can deduct commuting costs. Not claiming these means losing real money.

### 5. Wrong marital filing status

Choosing between individual and joint filing matters. Sometimes joint is better, sometimes not. It's worth calculating both ways.

## German Tax Deadlines

The deadline for filing your tax return is usually **31 July of the following year**. If you use a tax advisor (Steuerberater), you have until the end of February of the year after that.

**Important**: if you're owed a refund, you can file even after the deadline. In that case, no penalties apply. However, the tax office may refuse the return if more than 4 years have passed since the end of the tax year.

This means that in 2025, you can still file for: 2021, 2022, 2023, and 2024. The year 2020 expires if you haven't filed yet.
