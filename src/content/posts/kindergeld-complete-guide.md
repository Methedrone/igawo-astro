# Kindergeld Complete Guide – German Child Benefit Explained

If you worked in Germany and have children, you're probably entitled to Kindergeld. It's a German monthly child benefit currently set at EUR 250 per child. That's EUR 3,000 per year per child – money that many foreign families simply miss out on because they don't know their rights.

In this article, I explain step by step who can get Kindergeld, how to apply, what to do if you're rejected, and what pitfalls await parents in the German system.

## What Is Kindergeld?

Kindergeld is a state family allowance in Germany. It goes to parents who live or work in Germany and raise children there. You don't need to be a German citizen – it's enough that you worked legally in Germany and paid social security contributions.

The amount is fixed and doesn't depend on your income. Whether you earned EUR 1,500 or EUR 4,000 per month, you get the same amount for each child.

**Kindergeld rates for 2024/2025:**

- First and second child: EUR 250 per month
- Third child: EUR 250 per month
- Fourth and each additional child: EUR 250 per month

In addition, there is a supplementary child allowance (Kinderzuschlag) for low-income families, but more on that later.

## Who Qualifies for Kindergeld?

Parents who meet one of these conditions can claim:

- Employed in Germany under an employment contract
- Self-employed in Germany
- Receiving unemployment benefits (Arbeitslosengeld)
- Citizens of the EU, EEA, or Switzerland with freedom of movement

**Important**: if you're a Polish citizen (or any EU citizen) and worked in Germany, you have full entitlement to Kindergeld. As an EU member, there are no additional restrictions.

### Child's age

Kindergeld applies to children up to 18 years old. Exceptions:

- Up to 21 years old – if the child is registered as unemployed
- Up to 25 years old – if the child is in education (school, university, vocational training)

The child must live in Germany, another EU country, or Switzerland. If the child lives in Poland, you can still get Kindergeld, but conditions are slightly different.

## Kindergeld When Your Child Lives Abroad

This is one of the most common cases among foreign parents working in Germany. The children stay with mom in Poland while dad works in Munich or Berlin. Does Kindergeld still apply?

**Yes, it does.** If you work in Germany and your child lives in Poland, you are entitled to Kindergeld. But additional conditions apply:

1. You must pay social security contributions in Germany
2. Your income must be high enough that your home country doesn't have priority for paying the benefit
3. You must apply through the German Familienkasse

In practice, this means that if you earn more than around EUR 900 per month in Germany, you typically have the right to German Kindergeld even if your child lives in Poland.

### Difference between Kindergeld and your home country's child benefit

Your home country's child benefit program and German Kindergeld are two separate benefits. You can receive both simultaneously, provided you meet the conditions for both.

But beware: if you receive family benefits from your home country, the German office may offset them against your Kindergeld. This doesn't always happen – it depends on your individual situation. It's worth checking carefully to avoid problems.

## How to Apply for Kindergeld

You submit your application to the Familienkasse assigned to your health insurer. If you were insured with AOK, you apply to Familienkasse AOK. If TK, then Familienkasse TK.

### Step 1: Find the correct office

The easiest way is to call your German health insurer and ask which Familienkasse handles your case. You can also check online, but calling is faster.

### Step 2: Get the forms

The application consists of several parts:

- **Zusammenfassender Antrag KG 1–6** – main application
- **Anlage Kind** – attachment with child's details
- **Anlage Ausländisches Einkommen** – if you have foreign income
- **Anlage Erklärung zum Arbeitslohn** – earnings data

Forms are in German. If you don't know the language, it's better to let professionals handle it. One wrong checkbox and the application comes back with a request for clarification.

### Step 3: Gather documents

You'll need:

- Copy of passport or ID card
- Child's birth certificate (with certified German translation)
- Tax identification number (Steuer-ID)
- Proof of residence in Germany (if applicable)
- Home-country documents – if the child lives there
- Lohnabrechnungen or earnings certificate

### Step 4: Submit the application

Send the completed application by post to the correct Familienkasse. There is no online application option for foreign citizens. Post is the only way.

## What to Do If You Get Rejected

Rejections from Familienkasse are common. Often they result from formal errors rather than lack of entitlement. Here are the most common reasons:

### Missing documents

Familienkasse may reject you if you don't provide all required attachments. The problem is that requests for documents often get lost in the mail. Check your mailbox regularly.

### Errors in the application

Wrong numbers, wrong dates, illegible handwriting – all reasons for rejection. German bureaucracy is unforgiving.

### Missing proof of education

If the child is over 18, Familienkasse requires proof that the child is still studying. A school certificate from your home country must be translated into German.

### Income too low

In some cases, when income in Germany is very low and home-country benefits are high, Familienkasse may decide your home country has priority for payment.

## How to Appeal a Rejection

If you receive a rejection, it's not the end. You have the right to file an objection (Widerspruch). The deadline is usually one month from receiving the decision.

In your objection, you must:

- Explain why you believe the decision is wrong
- Provide evidence (missing documents, clarifications)
- Reference specific legal provisions

Most rejections are overturned on appeal when:

- You supply missing documents
- You clarify ambiguities in the application
- You show that you meet all conditions

**Tip**: don't send an appeal yourself if you don't know German tax law. An improperly worded objection can close the door to further appeals.

## Is Kindergeld Taxable?

No, Kindergeld is tax-free. You don't need to declare it in your German tax return or your home-country tax return. It's a family benefit, not taxable income.

But note: Kindergeld affects the child tax allowance (Kinderfreibetrag). In your tax return, the office automatically calculates whether Kindergeld or Kinderfreibetrag is better for you. Usually Kindergeld is more favorable, but for higher incomes Kinderfreibetrag may be better.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does Kindergeld apply to children from a previous marriage?

Yes, if you raise or support them. It doesn't matter whether you're the biological parent.

### Does Kindergeld apply if the child studies abroad?

Yes, if studies are in an EU or EEA country. Studies in Poland, Czechia, Hungary, or Italy are no problem.

### Can Kindergeld be backdated?

Yes, but no further than 6 months before the application date. If you apply in July, you'll get Kindergeld from January.

### How long does processing take?

From a few weeks to a few months. In complicated cases (child abroad, previous rejections), it can take up to half a year.

## Summary

Kindergeld is real support that thousands of foreign families are entitled to. Key points:

- Kindergeld applies to each child up to 18 years old (or 25 if in education)
- You don't need to live in Germany – working there is enough
- The benefit is EUR 250 per month per child
- You apply through the Familienkasse assigned to your health insurer
- If rejected, you have the right to appeal
- Kindergeld is tax-free
